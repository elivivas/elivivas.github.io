WomenResID

The Ladies' Residence, the female group of the Students' Residence founded in 1915 in Madrid under the direction of María de Maeztu, was the first official institution in Spain dedicated to higher education for women. Promoted by the Board for Extension of Studies (JAE), and inspired by the ideals of the Free Institution of Education (ILE), its main objective was to provide women with access to quality education and to promote their intellectual and personal development. At WomenResID, we have extracted and analyzed data from the student registration books with the nearly 3,000 women who resided in or visited the Residence combined with those from various related sources with the aim of making them visible. Pioneer women, many anonymous, who paved the way for equal higher education, an initiative that was cut short in 1936 by the outbreak of the civil war.

Background

Fernando de Castro creates the Association for Women's Education

In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, Spain experienced changes in education and women's rights, driven by feminist associations and reform movements that challenged the exclusion of women from higher education.
The Free Institution of Education (ILE), founded in 1876 by Francisco Giner de los Ríos, was key to promoting a modern and secular education that included women. Although initially few women managed to enter university, facing strong resistance, these advances prepared the ground for the creation of the Ladies' Residence.

The Students' Residence was founded in 1910. Five years later, its female group, the Ladies' Residence, was born. It was an initiative of the Board for Extension of Studies and Scientific Research (JAE) and became an important cultural and educational center.

Women at the Residence

Residents in the library of the Ladies' Residence at Miguel Ángel, 8, around 1930. International Institute in Spain

Women who passed
through the Residence

The Ladies' Residence experienced remarkable growth from its foundation in 1915 until 1936. During these years, it became an important training center and home for residents enrolled in different educational institutions in Madrid. Women from middle and upper-middle class, and even some helped with scholarships and "honor loans" in the later years, came to take courses or study middle and higher education. The number of residents grew significantly, directly correlating with the number of women accessing university education. According to researcher Raquel Vázquel Ramil (2012), in the 1914-1915 academic year there were 90 female university students enrolled throughout Spain, while in 1932-1933 the number rose to 2,047, with the Central University of Madrid having the highest percentage of enrolled female students, representing 34.4% of the total in 1915. Even so, in the 1930s, only 6% of those enrolled in university were women.

It attracted women from almost
all areas of the country

The residents came from all parts of Spain, although the largest group came from León. The management of the Residence took advantage of the strong imprint and values of the Free Institution of Education (ILE) in León, promoted by the Sierra Pambley Foundation. The Krausist and institutionalist influence, through, among others, Gumersindo de Azcárate, encouraged many progressive families in the region to send their daughters to the Residence, resulting in a considerable number of students from León at the institution.

The international focus

Foreign residents represented 13% of the total number of women (more than 400 residents between 1915 and 1936). The relationship between the Ladies' Residence of Madrid and American women's colleges between 1919 and 1936 was very fruitful. Student exchange policies were established that promoted the cultural and academic enrichment of both parties, based on solidarity and mutual support, with a focus on modernizing women's education and offering them international opportunities. Founded by women and for women, they provided a model of advanced and autonomous education.
The female professors actively promoted scholarships and exchanges, which allowed many Spanish women to study in the United States and vice versa. In addition, these policies helped introduce modern educational methods in Spain, such as the emphasis on experimental sciences, languages, and physical education.
Susan Huntington, professor at the International Institute
The importance of the presence
of foreign women

An example of this involvement was that of Susan Huntington, who promoted educational exchange between Spain and the United States in its earliest phase. From 1917 onwards, she decisively supported the collaboration of the International Institute with the JAE in the Instituto-Escuela and the Ladies' Residence. In 1932, she received a tribute from the Spanish government for her "material and moral" support for the foundation of the Ladies' Residence. In this ceremony, held at Fortuny 53 —at that time the headquarters of the Ladies' Residence and previously of the International Institute (1903-1927)—, the building was baptized with the name of Casa Susana Huntington.

Most were European
and American

Foreign women came to the Ladies' Residence mainly to study Spanish and came primarily from the United States, but there were also students from other European countries such as France, Germany, and the United Kingdom. This exchange not only enriched the academic environment but also fostered an international network of professional and academic women.

Dorotea Barnés González working in the Laboratory
A pioneer in the study of Chemistry

Dorotea Barnés González, born in 1904 in Pamplona, was one of the Spanish pioneers in Chemistry, both in research and teaching, during the Silver Age. A doctor and high school teacher, her research work was key in the incorporation of techniques such as Raman spectroscopy to chemistry departments in Spain. She was one of the women who benefited from the educational opportunities provided by the Ladies' Residence. Although not a resident, she trained at the Foster Laboratory and was awarded a scholarship by the JAE in 1929, at Smith College in Northampton, Massachusetts, where she specialized in research in the field of spectral analysis techniques, under the direction of Gladys Anslow, Physics PhD from Yale University and professor at the Chemistry Department of Smith College.

Pedagogical innovation

Hockey team of the Ladies' Residence, thirties. International Institute in Spain

The hub: from
international colleges to
pedagogical vanguard


The pedagogical system of the Ladies' Residence, inspired by international colleges, especially British and American models, was based on promoting a comprehensive education that combined academic development with personal and social training. The Ladies' Residence became what today would be called a hub, a nucleus of interdisciplinary exchange that drove the development of its residents. They not only attended university classes but also participated in a variety of extracurricular activities that included conferences, workshops, sports, and cultural activities. The idea was to foster an environment of continuous and multidisciplinary learning, where students could develop critical and social skills, in addition to their academic knowledge.
The Residence also promoted coexistence and the exchange of ideas between students and teachers, creating an environment of community and mutual support. This holistic approach sought to empower women, preparing them to be competent professionals and active citizens in society.
In summary, the pedagogical system of the Ladies' Residence was innovative, secular, and progressive, seeking to form independent women prepared to face the challenges of their time.
